The Taj Mahal is an iconic white marble mausoleum located in Agra, Uttar Pradesh, India. Commissioned by the Mughal emperor Shah Jahan in memory of his beloved wife Mumtaz Mahal, it stands as one of the most renowned symbols of love and architectural beauty in the world. Construction of the Taj Mahal began in 1632 and was completed in 1653, employing thousands of skilled artisans, laborers, and craftsmen.

The mausoleum's architecture is a stunning blend of Persian, Islamic, and Indian influences, showcasing intricate details and exquisite craftsmanship. The central structure is a large, white marble dome surrounded by four minarets, creating a symmetrical and harmonious design. The main building houses the tombs of Shah Jahan and Mumtaz Mahal in a beautifully adorned chamber.

The Taj Mahal is set within a vast complex featuring lush gardens, reflecting pools, and additional structures such as the mosque and the guesthouse. The complex is laid out in a perfect geometric pattern, emphasizing the precision and balance characteristic of Mughal garden design.

Designated as a UNESCO World Heritage Site in 1983, the Taj Mahal attracts millions of visitors each year who come to marvel at its breathtaking architecture, learn about its historical significance, and witness the changing hues of its marble facade throughout the day. Additionally, the Taj Mahal is a symbol of enduring love and has become an integral part of India's cultural and historical heritage.
